The Challenge

Students were making life-defining decisions — and didn't trust the AI helping them

DreamCollege AI had strong capabilities underneath — but the interface gave users no window into how recommendations were generated. Students felt anxious making major decisions based on opaque suggestions from an AI they didn't understand.

Drop-off happened at two critical moments: during onboarding (too many questions, low perceived value) and after the first AI interaction (no sense of why the AI said what it said).

How Might We: Design an AI counseling experience that students genuinely trust — not because we tell them to, but because every interaction earns it?
  • High early drop-off — students left before experiencing value
  • Zero explainability — recommendations felt arbitrary
  • Generic tone — AI didn't adapt to different student profiles
  • No progress feedback — users didn't know where they stood
  • Mobile experience was an afterthought — most students are mobile-first
  • Parents and counselors excluded from the loop despite heavy influence

"I don't understand why it recommended these schools. How does it know what's right for me?"

— Sophomore student, user interview

Solution

Transparent AI, progressive trust, and a design system built to scale

We redesigned the end-to-end experience around a single principle: trust must be earned in every interaction. That meant inline AI reasoning, progressive onboarding, adaptive conversational flows, and a 48-component design system that gave the whole team a shared language for building fast without breaking quality.

A $591.1B market with a 70% trust problem

Traditional college counseling costs $150–$500/hour — accessible only to the top income bracket. DreamCollege AI existed to democratize that guidance. But it could only do that if students actually trusted it.

Research Insight

Three trust barriers that shaped everything

Across 35 interviews, three patterns kept surfacing — each requiring a distinct design response. Naming them gave the whole team a shared framework for every decision.

01
Black Box Anxiety
Students couldn't see how recommendations were made. Without reasoning, even good advice felt arbitrary. The fix: inline "why" explanations in natural language alongside every AI suggestion.
02
Comparison Trap
Students constantly compared themselves to peers — feeling behind if the AI suggested "reach" schools. The fix: reframing recommendations around personal fit and trajectory, not rankings.
03
Emotional Stakes ≠ Logical Stakes
College choice is a logical decision with enormous emotional weight. Pure data felt cold; pure empathy felt patronizing. The fix: an adaptive AI tone that shifted between mentor and coach modes.

Design Principles

Three principles that drove every decision

01
Transparent by Default
Every AI action should be legible. Show the reasoning. Make the invisible visible. If the AI can't explain it, it shouldn't say it.
02
Human-Centered AI
The AI is a tool for the student — not the other way around. Every interaction should feel like support, not surveillance. The human is always in control.
03
Progressive Trust
Trust is earned incrementally. Don't ask for commitment before delivering value. Every screen should give before it takes.

Key Design Challenge

Redesigning the conversational AI — from 31% drop-off to 7%

The original AI chat interface had a 31% drop-off rate at the first response. Students were hitting the AI's reply and leaving — not because the advice was bad, but because the format felt robotic, cold, and uncertain.

I ran 12 prototype tests with progressively warmer, more structured response formats. The winning version: shorter paragraphs, inline reasoning callouts, confidence indicators, and a "tell me more" follow-up prompt that kept the conversation alive.

  • Broke dense AI responses into digestible chunks with clear hierarchy
  • Added a "Why this school?" inline callout for every recommendation
  • Introduced confidence signals ("Strong match · 94% fit") to make probability legible
  • Designed follow-up prompts that felt like a real counselor probing deeper
  • Built streaming response animation so the AI felt "thinking" not "loading"

"This feels like it actually read my profile. It's not just throwing schools at me — it's explaining why they fit me specifically."

— Usability session participant, after redesigned AI interface

Stakeholder Management

The CEO wanted features. The research said: fix trust first.

Three weeks in, the CEO pushed to add a new feature — a college comparison tool — before we'd fixed the foundational trust issues. His logic: "More features = more value." My research said the opposite.

I created a "Trust-First Roadmap" that visualized the dependency chain: if students don't trust the AI's current suggestions, a comparison tool will just give them more suggestions they don't trust. I made the data undeniable — and we kept the focus.

The outcome: CEO approved a 6-week trust-first sprint before any new feature work. The metrics validated the call — retention jumped 63% before we shipped a single new feature.

The lesson: Stakeholder alignment isn't about winning arguments. It's about making the data so clear that the right decision becomes obvious. Frame around business outcomes, not design preferences.

Unexpected Insights

Three things research revealed that we didn't expect

01
Students Don't Read
Even motivated students scanned, not read. Long AI responses — no matter how accurate — got scrolled past. The solution wasn't better writing. It was better chunking, hierarchy, and progressive disclosure.
02
Parents Are Hidden Users
Students routinely showed the AI's recommendations to their parents — who then influenced whether they acted on them. Designing only for students meant ignoring the actual decision-maker in many households.
03
Anxiety Drives Behavior
Session recordings showed students repeatedly refreshing the same recommendation as if it would change. This wasn't confusion — it was anxiety. We added a "Your profile hasn't changed — here's why this still fits you" reassurance pattern.

Design System

48 components. One source of truth. Zero design drift.

With four developers and four UX designers working in parallel, design inconsistency was the biggest risk to product quality. I built a design system from scratch — not just a component library, but a shared language for the whole team.

The system used semantic tokens (color, spacing, typography) so that dark mode, responsive breakpoints, and brand updates could be applied globally in one change. Every component was documented with usage guidelines and accessibility notes.

  • 48 components covering every product surface
  • Semantic token architecture — color, spacing, and type tokens
  • Strict naming conventions agreed with dev team (BEM-inspired)
  • Component documentation with do/don't usage examples
  • 45% reduction in design-to-development handoff time
  • Zero "what does this Figma annotation mean?" Slack messages by week 8

Reflections

What I'd do differently — and what I'm proud of

What I'd Do Differently

  • Involve parents earlier as a distinct user group — their influence was underestimated until week 5
  • Run quantitative research alongside qualitative from day one — some of our assumptions held for weeks before data contradicted them
  • Establish the design system before the first production component, not alongside it — retrofitting tokens is painful

What I'm Proud Of

  • Turning "trust" — an abstract concept — into a measurable, designable property that the whole team could act on
  • The CEO conversation: holding the research line and being proven right by the metrics
  • Building a design system that developers genuinely used and praised — not just a Figma file that lived in a link nobody clicked

What I Learned

Three things this project taught me

01
Trust is designed, not assumed
Users don't trust AI because it's capable — they trust it when the interface makes reasoning legible. Explainability is a design feature, not an engineering afterthought.
02
Reduce before you add
The biggest win was cutting complexity before adding features. Simplification required more design judgment than building new flows — and delivered more measurable impact.
03
Emotion is a UX signal
Students making life-defining decisions brought anxiety into every session. Designing for emotional state — not just task completion — was what made this product genuinely different.
