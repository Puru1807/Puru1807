01
Overview

When the machine is slower than the human — a fixable problem

Self-checkout kiosks promise speed and independence but often deliver frustration. Safeway's existing kiosk experience had accumulated years of incremental additions without a holistic UX review — resulting in a system that violated multiple foundational usability principles and regularly required staff intervention.

This project applied Nielsen's 10 Heuristics as the analytical framework, identifying severity-rated violations and redesigning each flow from the ground up. The result: a system that actually fulfills the promise of self-checkout — faster, less error-prone, and accessible without assistance.

02
Audit

18 violations across 10 principles — catalogued and severity-rated

Before designing a single new screen, I conducted a structured evaluation of the existing kiosk interface against Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics. Each violation was documented, rated for severity (1–4 scale), and prioritized for redesign.

Heuristic Violation Found Severity
Visibility of system status No feedback when item is successfully scanned — users re-scan, causing duplicates High
Match between system and real world Technical error codes ("Error 4201") with no plain-language explanation High
User control and freedom No clear way to remove a scanned item — users had to call staff High
Consistency and standards Buttons for the same action labeled differently across screens ("Done", "Finish", "Complete") Medium
Error prevention No confirmation before payment — accidental submissions with wrong amounts High
Recognition over recall Item list hidden behind scroll — users unsure what's in their cart Medium
Flexibility and efficiency No loyalty card fast-track — frequent shoppers go through same flow as first-timers Low
Aesthetic and minimalist design 14 elements on the main screen — visual noise causes decision paralysis Medium

03
Process

Evaluate, prioritize, redesign — one heuristic at a time

The redesign process followed the evaluation findings strictly — no "while we're at it" scope creep. High-severity violations were redesigned first; medium violations were addressed in the same pass where they intersected with high-severity flows; low-severity issues were documented for a future iteration.

01
Evaluate
Walked through all kiosk flows against all 10 heuristics. Documented 18 violations with screenshots. Rated severity 1–4 per Nielsen's scale.
02
Prioritize
Ranked violations by severity × frequency × fix complexity. Identified 4 critical flows to redesign: scan, cart review, error recovery, and payment.
03
Redesign
High-fidelity kiosk screens for each critical flow. Designed for touchscreen constraints: large tap targets (minimum 44×44dp), high contrast, glanceable information hierarchy.
04
Validate
Moderated usability testing with 8 participants. Timed task completion. Compared error rates and time-on-task against the original system baseline.
04
Solution

A kiosk that works with human behavior, not against it

The redesign addressed every high-severity violation directly. The guiding principle: at a checkout kiosk, every extra second and every confusion moment has a real-world cost — a longer queue, an abandoned transaction, a staff call. The interface should feel faster than a human cashier, not slower.

  • Instant scan feedback: Green checkmark + item name appears within 200ms of scan. Cart total updates live. No doubt about whether the scan registered.
  • Always-visible cart: Persistent side panel shows all scanned items — no scrolling, no hiding. Inline "remove" button on each item — no staff needed.
  • Plain-language errors: "This item needs a cashier to verify your age" instead of "Error 4201." Specific next step on every error screen.
  • Pre-payment summary: Full cart review screen before any payment action — with one-tap edit for any item. Prevents accidental submissions.
  • Reduced visual noise: Main scan screen reduced from 14 to 4 primary elements. Progressive disclosure for secondary options (coupons, loyalty, help).
  • Accessibility: All text at minimum 18pt on kiosk screens. High contrast ratios. Large touch zones. Audio feedback toggle.

05
Results

Faster, fewer errors, fewer abandoned checkouts

Usability testing against the original system showed improvements across all measured tasks. Average checkout time dropped 40%. Staff-call incidents in test sessions fell to zero — every error was recoverable without assistance. The 84% transaction completion rate (up from 61% baseline) reflects the direct impact of addressing error prevention and user control heuristics.

06
Reflection

Retail UX is high-stakes, time-pressured design

01
Error prevention beats error recovery
The biggest kiosk frustrations weren't complex — they were preventable. Clear affordances, better weight sensor feedback, and pre-emptive guidance reduced errors before they happened.
02
Heuristics are a starting point, not a conclusion
The heuristic evaluation surfaced problems efficiently, but contextual observation revealed the real pain: time pressure, embarrassment of errors, and anxiety about holding up the line. Frameworks miss emotion.
03
Designing for varied literacy levels
Self-checkout serves everyone — from tech-native shoppers to elderly users who've never used a touchscreen. Every design decision had to work across the widest possible range without a help button.
04
Physical context changes everything
Designing for a kiosk means accounting for ambient noise, glare, social pressure from queues, and physical fatigue. Standard screen design assumptions don't transfer cleanly to in-store contexts.

Conclusion

Safeway Kiosk showed me that great UX isn't about novelty — it's about removing friction that never should have been there. A principled methodology, applied rigorously, can fix years of accumulated debt.

The most impactful design changes were the simplest: tell users what happened, show them their cart, let them fix mistakes. Fundamentals, done well.
